Physics Study Guide - 1st Trimester

1. What is Physics & Science?

What is Physics?

Physics is the natural science that studies matter, energy, motion, and force. Its main goal is to understand the fundamental laws of the universe.

Key Areas of Physics:

  • Mechanics: The study of motion and forces (what we are studying now!).
  • Thermodynamics: The study of heat, energy, and work.
  • Electromagnetism: The study of electric and magnetic fields.
  • Optics: The study of light.

What is Science?

Science is a systematic way of understanding the natural world based on observation, experimentation, and evidence.

The 3 Branches of Science:

1. Natural Sciences
Study nature and physical phenomena. (e.g., Physics, Chemistry, Biology).
2. Formal Sciences
Use logic, reasoning, and symbols. They are the "language" of science. (e.g., Mathematics, Computer Science).
3. Social Sciences
Study human behavior and societies. (e.g., Psychology, Sociology).

Science vs. Pseudoscience

It's important to know the difference!

  • Science: Follows the Scientific Method, requires evidence, is testable, and is open to peer review and change.
  • Pseudoscience: Claims to be scientific but lacks evidence and does not follow the scientific method. Claims are often vague and cannot be proven false.

Examples of Pseudoscience: Astrology, Homeopathy, Numerology. These are not supported by scientific evidence.

2. The Scientific Method

What is it?

The Scientific Method is the structured process scientists use to investigate questions and solve problems. It ensures that conclusions are based on evidence, not just opinions.

The 6 Steps

You need to know these steps in order!

  1. Observation / Question: Noticing something or asking a question about the natural world. (e.g., "Why does my plant grow taller near the window?")
  2. Research: Gathering information about the question.
  3. Hypothesis: A testable prediction or a possible explanation. It's often written as an "If... then..." statement. (e.g., "If I put the plant in sunlight, then it will grow taller.")
  4. Experiment: Designing and carrying out a test to see if the hypothesis is correct.
  5. Analyze Data: Looking at the results of the experiment (measurements, observations) to see what they mean.
  6. Conclusion: A summary of what you learned. You state whether your hypothesis was supported or rejected by the evidence.

Hypothesis vs. Theory vs. Law

Hypothesis
A testable prediction for a *single* experiment.
Theory
A well-tested explanation for *why* something happens, supported by a lot of evidence. (e.g., Theory of Relativity).
Law
A description of *what* happens in nature, often a mathematical formula. (e.g., Law of Universal Gravitation).

3. Units of Measurement (SI)

Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is anything that can be measured. (e.g., your height, the time it takes to run, the temperature outside).

International System of Units (SI)

This is the standard system of measurement used by scientists worldwide. It's like a universal language for measurement.

The 7 Fundamental Quantities

These are the basic building blocks. All other units are made from these seven.

  • Length - Unit: meter (m)
  • Mass - Unit: kilogram (kg)
  • Time - Unit: second (s)
  • Temperature - Unit: Kelvin (K)
  • Electric Current - Unit: Ampere (A)
  • Amount of Substance - Unit: mole (mol)
  • Luminous Intensity - Unit: candela (cd)

(For your test, definitely know the first five!)

Derived Quantities

These are quantities made by combining fundamental quantities.

Area
Formula: Length × Length. Unit: $m^2$ (square meters)
Volume
Formula: Length × Length × Length. Unit: $m^3$ (cubic meters)
Speed
Formula: Length / Time. Unit: m/s (meters per second)
Force
Formula: Mass × Acceleration. Unit: Newton (N)

SI Conversions

You must be able to convert between common units.

  • 1 kilometer (km) = 1000 meters (m)
  • 1 meter (m) = 100 centimeters (cm)
  • 1 meter (m) = 1000 millimeters (mm)
  • 1 kilogram (kg) = 1000 grams (g)
  • 1 hour = 60 minutes
  • 1 minute = 60 seconds
  • 1 hour = 3600 seconds (60 × 60)

Temperature Conversion (Important!)

The SI unit is Kelvin (K), but we often use Celsius (°C).

T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

Example: Convert 25°C to Kelvin.

T(K) = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K

4. Describing Motion: Scalars & Vectors

Scalar Quantities

A scalar is a quantity that has only magnitude (size or amount).

It tells you "how much" but NOT "which way."

Examples of Scalars:

  • Distance (e.g., 10 meters)
  • Speed (e.g., 25 m/s)
  • Mass (e.g., 5 kg)
  • Time (e.g., 15 seconds)
  • Temperature (e.g., 20°C)

Vector Quantities

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude AND direction.

It tells you "how much" AND "which way."

Examples of Vectors:

  • Displacement (e.g., 10 meters East)
  • Velocity (e.g., 25 m/s North)
  • Force (e.g., 10 Newtons downward)
  • Acceleration (e.g., 9.8 m/s² downward)

Distance vs. Displacement

This is a key concept that uses scalars and vectors.

  • Distance (Scalar): The total path you traveled. (e.g., "My watch says I walked 5,000 steps.")
  • Displacement (Vector): The straight-line change in position from your start point to your end point, including direction.

Example:

Imagine you walk 3 meters East, then turn around and walk 3 meters West.

  • Distance: 3 m + 3 m = 6 meters (You walked a total of 6 m).
  • Displacement: 0 meters (You ended up exactly where you started!).

5. Speed & Velocity

Speed (Scalar)

Speed is a scalar quantity that measures how fast an object is moving. It's the rate at which an object covers distance.

Average Speed is the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken.

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time
  • Unit: meters per second (m/s)
  • This tells you "how fast" only.

Velocity (Vector)

Velocity is a vector quantity that measures the rate of change of displacement. It is speed with a direction.

Average Velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time taken.

Average Velocity = Total Displacement / Total Time
  • Unit: meters per second (m/s), plus a direction (e.g., "10 m/s North").
  • This tells you "how fast" and "which way."

Key Example (Speed vs. Velocity)

A runner runs 400 meters around a circular track in 80 seconds, ending at their starting point.

Find the Average Speed:

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time

Average Speed = 400 m / 80 s = 5 m/s

Find the Average Velocity:

Average Velocity = Total Displacement / Total Time

The runner ended where they started, so their displacement is 0 m.

Average Velocity = 0 m / 80 s = 0 m/s

Uniform Linear Motion (ULM)

This is the simplest type of motion.

  • Uniform: Means constant velocity (speed and direction do not change).
  • Linear: Means motion in a straight line.

Example: A car driving at exactly 50 km/h on a perfectly straight road.
